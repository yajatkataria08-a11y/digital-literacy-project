# Case Study: Business Email Compromise (BEC) Fraud
### The 2019 Toyota Boshoku Corporation Attack

---

## What Is the Crime?

Business Email Compromise (BEC) is a financially motivated cybercrime in which attackers
impersonate trusted executives or vendors via email to trick employees into transferring
large sums of money to fraudulent accounts.

---

## How It Typically Happens (Step-by-Step)

1. **Reconnaissance** – Attackers research the target company's hierarchy, key personnel,
   and vendor relationships using LinkedIn, company websites, and public filings.
2. **Email Spoofing / Account Takeover** – They either spoof a legitimate executive's email
   address or compromise a real account through phishing.
3. **The Request** – A convincing email is sent to a finance employee urgently requesting a
   wire transfer, often citing a time-sensitive deal or confidential acquisition.
4. **Transfer Executed** – The employee, believing the request is legitimate, wires funds to
   an attacker-controlled account.
5. **Money Mule Chain** – Funds are quickly moved through multiple accounts across
   jurisdictions, making recovery nearly impossible.

---

## Who Is Usually Targeted?

- Finance and accounts-payable employees
- Mid-to-large corporations with complex supply chains
- Companies undergoing mergers, acquisitions, or large procurement cycles

---

## The Incident

In September 2019, Toyota Boshoku Corporation — a key supplier in Toyota's global supply
chain — lost approximately **$37 million USD** after an employee was deceived into changing
bank account details for a wire transfer. Attackers impersonated a business partner and
applied social pressure to rush the transaction through without standard verification.

---

## Consequences

- **Financial:** $37 million lost; partial recovery efforts launched through international
  law enforcement.
- **Operational:** Internal audit and approval processes overhauled company-wide.
- **Reputational:** Public disclosure damaged investor and partner confidence.
- **Legal:** Regulatory scrutiny over inadequate internal financial controls.

---

## Key Takeaway

No technical exploit was needed — only deception. BEC attacks succeed by exploiting
**human trust**, urgency, and gaps in verification procedures.
